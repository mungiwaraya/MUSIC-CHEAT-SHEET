Producer Tip & Genre Notes

SEC 07

Chords To Avoid Together (Clash & Dissonance Guide)

Short Notes: While music rules are made to be broken creatively, certain chord pairings and interval leaps produce unwanted acoustic mud, harmonic confusion, or genre-breaking dissonance.
Chord Pair / Harmonic Interaction Acoustic Reason To Avoid Better Studio Alternative
Major I + Minor i (Simultaneous) Clashing minor 2nd interval between Major 3rd and Minor 3rd. Use Major I then transition to Minor iv (borrowed chord) for emotional impact.
Tritone Jump (e.g., C Major to F# Major without resolution) Extreme harmonic instability and jarring dissonance. Insert a pivot chord like D7 or Ab Major to smoothly resolve the tritone.
Low-Register Minor 2nd Intervals (e.g., C1 + Db1 in sub-bass) Creates severe low-end phase cancellation and muddy rumble. Keep sub-bass strictly monophonic and play intervals above C3 (130 Hz).
Diminished vii° + Major I (Without V7 transition) Diminished triad sounds weak and abruptly resolved without dominant weight. Use V7 or V9 (which embeds the diminished triad) for a commanding resolution.
Parallel Dominant 7ths (e.g., C7 to D7 to E7 in clean pop) Sounds aimless and blues/jazz heavy; disrupts clean pop tonality. Use diatonic triads (C → Dm → Em) or resolve secondary dominants properly.
Minor v + Dominant V7 simultaneously Natural 7th and raised leading tone clash harshly. Choose either natural minor v for chill vibes or dominant V7 for dramatic resolution.

MIDI Guitar Realism Tip

When programming MIDI acoustic or electric guitar VSTs, never quantize notes to start exactly simultaneously. Strumming takes 15–35ms across strings—delay higher strings slightly for authentic pick action.

SEC 19

Final Advice (Studio Manifesto & Golden Rules)

Short Notes: Print this single-page studio manifesto and paste it beside your studio monitors. These foundational principles separate amateur hobbyists from master music producers.

✓ THE DOs

  • Trust your ears over visual spectrum analyzer curves every single time.
  • Reference your mixes on smartphone speakers, cheap earbuds, and car stereos.
  • Commit to audio quickly so you spend less time tweaking plugins and more time composing.
  • Leave headroom (-6 dB peak) on your master bus before sending tracks to mastering.
  • Organize your sample library with clear tags so inspiration never gets slowed down by folder digging.

✗ THE DON'Ts

  • Don't mix soloed instruments for hours; no one listens to the snare drum in isolation.
  • Don't slap stereo wideners on low-end sub frequencies below 120Hz.
  • Don't add 10 plugins to a vocal when good gain staging and one great compressor can do the job.
  • Don't let endless sound design sessions distract you from writing strong chords and unforgettable melodies.
  • Don't compare your rough 30-minute demo directly to a mastered $50,000 commercial mix.

⚠️ COMMON MISTAKES

  • Over-compressing the master bus until all dynamic punch and breathing room are squashed.
  • Ignoring phase cancellation between layered kicks, snares, and sub basses.
  • Adding too many competing melodies simultaneously instead of creating call-and-response clarity.
  • Forgetting to tune 808 subs and drum samples to the diatonic key of the song.
  • Mixing at blasting volumes for hours, leading to ear fatigue and harsh treble mixing.

🌟 GOLDEN RULES & MINDSET

  • Less is more: If an instrument doesn't serve the rhythm, harmony, or emotional hook, mute it immediately.
  • Contrast creates emotion: Quiet verses make loud choruses explode; dark chords make bright resolutions shine.
  • The vocal is king: Everything in your mix must arrange itself around clarity, intelligibility, and emotion of the lead vocal.
  • Rhythm drives melody: A simple 3-note melody with an incredible syncopated groove beats a complex 16-note solo every time.
  • Your taste is your greatest asset. Tools and plugins can be bought by anyone, but your aesthetic choices define your signature sound.
  • Perfection is the enemy of finished art. Ship your beats, release your music, learn the lessons, and move on to the next track.
  • Stay curious. Study music theory not as a set of rigid laws that restrict you, but as a map of emotional shortcuts to translate what you hear in your head directly into your speakers.
